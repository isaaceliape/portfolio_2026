Behind sclp.co: Building Award-Winning UI Work

March 10, 2026 6 min read

sclp.co is the live record of a decade of commercial UI engineering — web apps, landing pages, rich animations, and CMS-driven experiences that shipped to millions of users. Here's what I learned building that kind of work consistently at a high level.

What "UI Engineering" Actually Means

The phrase gets used loosely, so let me be specific. UI engineering sits at the intersection of design and systems. It's not just implementing mockups — it's understanding why a transition should be 240ms instead of 400ms, why a particular easing curve feels natural on a scroll-driven animation, and why the DOM structure you choose affects both performance and accessibility at the same time.

The work on sclp.co spans agency campaigns, SaaS products, and brand sites. What connects all of it is a focus on craft at the implementation layer — the stuff users feel but rarely notice consciously.

Animations That Don't Get in the Way

The most common mistake with UI animation is overreach: motion that exists for its own sake rather than to communicate something. Every animation I ship answers at least one of these questions:

  • Does this tell the user where something came from or where it went?
  • Does this make a state change feel responsive and immediate?
  • Does this guide attention without demanding it?

In practice, that means preferring transform and opacity over properties that trigger layout, keeping durations under 300ms for micro-interactions, and always providing a prefers-reduced-motion fallback that still communicates the same information without movement.

CMS Integration at Pace

A lot of the work on sclp.co involves integrating with content management systems — Contentful, Sanity, Prismic, and bespoke backends. The pattern I keep returning to is a strict separation between data shape and rendering. I define the content model first, then build a presentation layer that handles every permutation cleanly: missing fields, long strings, empty arrays, rich text with unusual nesting.

Edge cases in CMS-driven UIs are where polish lives or dies. A truncated title that breaks a grid layout, a missing image that collapses a card — these reach production because they're hard to catch in a static design review. Defensive rendering patterns and visual regression tests are the answer.

Why the Awards Matter (and Why They Don't)

Cannes Lions, Awwwards, CSS Awards — these validate a certain kind of executional quality. I'm proud of them. But awards are a lagging indicator: they measure the perception of past work, not current ability. What I care about more is whether the work shipped on time, whether it held up under real traffic, and whether the team that maintained it after handoff could work with it comfortably.

The best work I've done was awarded and maintainable. Those two things are not in opposition — they require the same underlying discipline.
